The Secret to Silky Homemade Hummus

It's not the tahini brand — it's technique. The baking-soda trick and blending method that get you there.

Ask ten people why their homemade hummus never tastes like the version from a good Israeli restaurant, and nine of them will blame the tahini. The tahini matters, but it's rarely the real problem. The real problem is almost always the chickpeas — specifically, what you do to them before they ever reach the blender.

The Skins Are the Enemy

Every chickpea is wrapped in a thin skin that turns gritty once blended. Restaurant-style hummus is silky specifically because most of those skins have been removed before blending. You don't need to peel each chickpea by hand — there's a much faster way.

The Baking Soda Trick

1. If cooking from dried chickpeas (strongly preferred over canned for this dish), soak them overnight with a half teaspoon of baking soda added to the soaking water. The alkalinity starts breaking down the pectin in the skins before cooking even begins.

2. Cook them the same way — drain the soaked chickpeas, then simmer with another half teaspoon of baking soda added to the fresh cooking water. Within a few minutes you'll see the skins start to loosen and float free; the chickpeas themselves turn noticeably softer and more matte in color. Cook until they're falling-apart tender, well past the point most people stop — hummus rewards overcooked chickpeas far more than al dente ones.

3. Using canned chickpeas? Simmer them for 15–20 minutes in fresh water with the same half teaspoon of baking soda. It won't match dried chickpeas cooked from scratch, but it meaningfully improves texture over blending them straight from the can.

Blend the Tahini and Lemon First

Before the chickpeas go anywhere near the blender, process the tahini with lemon juice, garlic and ice water on their own until the mixture turns pale, thick and fluffy — this can take a full minute or two and looks briefly like it's seizing before it smooths out. This step builds the emulsion that gives hummus its body. Only once that base is silky do the warm, well-cooked chickpeas go in, blended until completely smooth. Adding cold water a tablespoon at a time while blending — rather than all at once — is what gets hummus to that whipped, cloud-like texture instead of a thick paste.

Chefaleh's kitchen note: we blend hummus far longer than feels necessary — a good two to three minutes on high — because the difference between "smooth" and "silky" almost always comes down to blend time, not a secret ingredient.

Skip the skins, overcook the chickpeas on purpose, build the tahini emulsion first, and blend longer than feels reasonable. Do those four things and the tahini brand stops mattering nearly as much as everyone assumes.
