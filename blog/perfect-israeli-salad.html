How to Make Perfect Israeli Salad

Knife skills, salting timing, and the small habits that separate a watery chop from a proper Israeli salad.

Israeli salad looks like the simplest thing on the table — tomato, cucumber, a little onion, a squeeze of lemon. And yet most home versions come out watery, unevenly cut, and wilted within twenty minutes. The ingredient list is short. The technique is where it actually gets made or broken.

Dice Size Is Not a Style Choice

A proper Israeli salad is cut small — closer to a fine dice than a chop, ideally 3–5mm per piece. This isn't about aesthetics. Smaller pieces mean more surface area contacting the dressing, so every bite carries lemon, oil and salt evenly instead of pooling in the bottom of the bowl. Use a sharp knife, not a food processor — the blade shears cleanly through tomato flesh, while a processor's blunt impact crushes cell walls and releases excess liquid before the salad even hits the bowl.

Salt at the Very End, Not the Beginning

Salt draws water out of cucumber and tomato through osmosis — that's exactly what you want in a Greek salad you'll eat within the hour, and exactly what ruins an Israeli salad meant to hold at a Shabbat table for a few hours. Season with salt only right before serving, never while prepping. If you're making it ahead, keep the dressed vegetables and the salt separate until minutes before the table.

Deseed the Watery Parts

Tomato gel and cucumber seeds hold most of the vegetable's water content. Scoop the seedy core out of the tomato before dicing, and consider halving and scraping cucumbers if they're the seedy variety (Persian or English cucumbers need less of this — their seeds are smaller and less watery, which is part of why they're the standard choice here). This single step prevents more sogginess than any amount of extra salt-draining time.

Dress With Fat First, Acid Second

Toss the diced vegetables with olive oil first, letting it coat every surface — this creates a light barrier that slows how quickly the lemon juice pulls moisture out. Add the lemon juice and herbs (parsley, mint, or both) right before serving, not during prep. Order matters more than most people assume: oil-then-acid holds its texture noticeably longer than acid-then-oil.

Chefaleh's kitchen note: we dice each batch to order rather than prepping it hours in advance — for a salad this simple, freshness at the moment of dicing is most of what separates a good version from a forgettable one.

None of this requires special equipment or rare ingredients — just a sharp knife, a little restraint with the salt, and dressing at the right moment. Get those three things right and the "simple" salad becomes the dish people ask for the recipe of.
